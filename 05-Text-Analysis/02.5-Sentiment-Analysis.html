Sentiment Analysis¬∂

In this lesson, we‚Äôre going to learn how to use VADER, an English-language sentiment analysis tool designed for use with social media texts. This tool helps to calculate whether texts expresses positive or negative sentiment.

What is ‚Äúsentiment‚Äù? According to VADER‚Äôs creators, C.J. Hutto and Eric Gilbert:

‚Äù Sentiment analysis, or opinion mining, is an active area of study in the field of natural language processing that analyzes people‚Äôs opinions, sentiments, evaluations, attitudes, and emotions via the computational treatment of subjectivity in text.‚Äù

-‚ÄúVADER: A Parsimonious Rule-based Model for Sentiment Analysis of Social Media Text,‚Äù C.J. Hutto Eric Gilbert

In this lesson, we will cover how to use VADER to:

  • Calculate sentiment for individual sentences, thousands of tweets, and a short story

  • Create a plot of how sentiment fluctuates over time or throughout a text

Edward P. Jones‚Äôs Lost in the City (1993) is a collection of 14 short stories set in Washington D.C. The first short story, ‚ÄúThe Girl Who Raised Pigeons,‚Äù begins with a young girl raising homing pigeons on her roof.

How distinctive is a ‚Äúpigeon‚Äù in the world of Lost in the City? What does this uniqueness (or lackthereof) tell us about the meaning of pigeons in first short story ‚ÄúThe Girl Who Raised Pigeons‚Äù and the collection as a whole? These are just a few of the questions that we‚Äôre going to try to answer with tf-idf.

The Backstory of VADER, and How It Works¬∂

VADER, which stands for Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner, calculates the sentiment of texts by referring to a lexicon of words that have been assigned sentiment scores as well as by using a handful of simple rules. This tool was designed by C.J. Hutto and Eric Gilbert, and you can read more about its purpose and creation in their published paper.

Here‚Äôs a summary version: VADER‚Äôs lexicon was created by enlisting 10 different people to rate thousands of words positively or negatively on a scale of -4 to 4 (you can scroll through the lexicon on GitHub and check it out for yourself ‚Äî each line consists of a word, the mean rating, standard deviation, and 10 individual human ratings).

For example, you can see that the word crappy has a mean rating of -2.5 and the word bliss has a mean rating of 2.7. If you look at the 10 individual ratings, you can see that one person rated bliss as a 4, and another person rated bliss as a 1. This raises the question: just how ‚Äúpositive‚Äù is the word bliss? What do you think?

What about cutesie? It has a mean rating of 1, but two people rated it as a -1, and five people rated as a 2. Is cutesie an admiring adjective ‚Äî ‚ÄúShe was so cutesie I just had to talk to her‚Äù ‚Äî or a diminutive slight ‚Äî ‚ÄúUgh, her apartment was too cutesie‚Äù?

These difficult questions come up repeatedly when you read through the lexicon. Of course, VADER is designed to generalize, and it is not expected to capture the nuances of every single text. At the same time, it‚Äôs important to keep these questions close in mind whenever we use sentiment analysis tools: Does it matter that we‚Äôre missing out on specific nuances? What exactly are we measuring with this tool? Is this the best tool for the job?

In addition to its lexcion, VADER also calculates sentiment by considering 5 relatively simple rules:

  1. If there‚Äôs punctuation, especially exclamation points, the sentiment intensity should be increased (e.g., ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is bliss‚Äù üëç vs ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is bliss!!!‚Äù üëçüëçüëç )

  1. If there‚Äôs capitalization, especially all caps, the sentiment intensity should be increased (e.g., ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is bliss‚Äù üëç vs ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is BLISS‚Äù üëçüëçüëç )

  1. If there are words like ‚Äúextremely‚Äù or ‚Äúabsolutely‚Äù, the sentiment should be increased (‚ÄúMochi ice cream is good‚Äù üëç vs ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is extremely good‚Äù üëçüëçüëç )

  1. If there‚Äôs a ‚Äúbut‚Äù in a sentence, the polarity of the sentiment should shift, and the sentiment that follows the ‚Äúbut‚Äù should be prioritized (e.g., ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is nice‚Äù üëç vs ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is nice, but it‚Äôs a little blah‚Äù üëé )

  1. If there‚Äôs a negation before an important word, the sentiment polarity should be flipped (‚ÄúMochi ice cream is my favorite‚Äù üëç vs ‚ÄúMochi ice cream is not my favorite‚Äù üëé )

Because VADER uses a lexicon and simple rules, it works very fast and doesn‚Äôt require any training or set up, unlike more sophisticated. machine learning approaches.
